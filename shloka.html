Shloka: Designing a Faith-Based Climate Game

Blending Hindu rituals and narratives with serious game mechanics to foster deeper reflection and engagement around climate change.

The Problem

Climate change education often fails to connect with people's spiritual or cultural identities, leading to disinterest or shallow engagement. While serious games have emerged as tools for awareness, they rarely tap into players’ lived traditions or ethical frameworks. At the same time, religious narratives, from global flood myths to teachings of stewardship, have long addressed humanity’s relationship with nature. This disconnect raised a crucial design question: can games combine spiritual insight and interactive storytelling to deepen reflection on climate change?

The Approach

I led the design of Shloka, a serious game that integrates Hindu rituals and narratives into its core gameplay to explore climate responsibility. Using a research-through-design (RtD) methodology, we co-designed with cultural stakeholders, conducted ethnographic fieldwork, and transformed traditional practices—like chanting, mudras, and verse reading—into playable mechanics. We conducted a mixed-methods study to compare Shloka with other climate games, measuring how faith-based narratives influence reflection, engagement, and interest in environmental themes.

Methodology

Our research-through-design process followed a structured, multi-phase approach combining ethnographic inquiry, ritual digitization, iterative prototyping, and mixed-method evaluation. Grounded in cultural sensitivity and the IGDA Climate SIG framework, this process enabled the design of Shloka—a serious game that engages players through religious narratives and embodied climate action.

Step 1: Ethnographic Exploration of Cultural Spaces

We conducted visual ethnographic studies at the Tirumala and Sabarimala temples, collecting over 400 photographs, 43 videos, and extensive field notes. This exploration focused on how temple authorities and devotees engaged with climate-conscious practices. Observations included renewable energy initiatives, waste management systems, and public messaging around environmental responsibility.

Step 2: Analysis of Ethnographic Data

Using a Constructivist Grounded Theory approach, we developed open, axial, and selective codes that revealed six primary ways Hindu practices engage with environmental stewardship. These findings informed both the narrative and interaction design of the game.

Step 4: Iterative Game Design and Integration of IGDA Framework

With initial explorations digitizing Hindu rituals and identifying their connection to climate change, our next challenge was to design a meaningful gameplay experience that respects religious values while promoting environmental responsibility. We adopted a co-design approach, working closely with religious scholars, cultural stakeholders, and playtesters. In our first iteration, inspired by climate role-playing games, players assumed the roles of Hindu deities and used digitized rituals—like chants and mudras—to restore ecosystems and combat pollution. However, early feedback from scholars raised critical concerns: portraying deities as vulnerable or controllable conflicted with religious beliefs about their omnipotence, and allowing players to control them was seen as disrespectful.

Early prototypes allowed players to control Hindu deities battling climate hazards. However, cultural stakeholders expressed concerns over the appropriateness of depicting deities as vulnerable or controllable. One scholar stated, In response, we introduced the child protagonist Shloka, who performs rituals and learns from the wisdom of deities rather than embodying them directly.

In response, we reframed the narrative around a new protagonist—Shloka, a chosen child guided by divine wisdom to restore the environment. This approach shifted player agency away from controlling deities and toward embodying a human agent of change, preserving both cultural sensitivity and design intent. As we further refined the gameplay, scholars highlighted that combat-focused mechanics conflicted with Hinduism’s non-violent, harmonious worldview. Additionally, playtests revealed that requiring rituals to gain "ammo" disrupted gameplay flow and shifted focus away from the climate change message. To address these issues, we repositioned deities as mentors rather than playable characters, guiding Shloka through reflective rituals that inspire climate action. This design choice aligned the experience with the IGDA Climate Special Interest Group’s Four-Stage Framework—Knowledge, Attitude, Efficacy, and Hope—to foster environmental awareness and actionable optimism. This iterative process resulted in a respectful and engaging serious game experience. Shloka successfully integrates cultural values, player agency, and educational goals, demonstrating how thoughtful UX research and stakeholder collaboration can navigate complex intersections of belief systems and behavior change.

Guided by the IGDA Climate SIG framework, each level in Shloka was structured around the four progressive stages of Knowledge, Attitude, Efficacy, and Hope, ensuring that players not only gained awareness but also developed emotional investment and a sense of agency.

Step 5: Game Evaluation, Playtesting and Benchmarking

To evaluate the effectiveness of Shloka, we conducted a comparative study against a curated corpus of climate change games. Our goal was to assess whether incorporating religious rituals as interaction mechanics improved player engagement, learning outcomes, and interest in climate change action.

Establishing a Baseline Corpus

We first established a baseline corpus of climate change serious games. This involved identifying games with diverse mechanics, ranging from quizzes and simulations to narrative-driven and AR/VR experiences. Games were classified based on their core player interactions, answering the question: "What action does the player perform to progress in the game?"

Through extensive searches across app stores, scholarly databases, and serious games repositories, we identified 41 relevant games. From these, we selected nine titles—each representing a distinct interaction type—for direct comparison with Shloka.

Understanding the Baseline

The games were categorized into nine distinct groups, including quiz-based games, simulation models, platformers, interactive narratives, AR/VR experiences, and hyper-casual titles. Each selected game emphasized a different type of player action, allowing us to explore how ritual-based interactions in Shloka compared to conventional mechanics in fostering engagement and interest in climate issues.

Measures

We evaluated gameplay experiences using validated instruments. To assess game engagement, we adopted the GAMEFULQUEST survey, which measures dimensions such as Accomplishment, Challenge, Playfulness, Social Connection, and Immersion. For player satisfaction, we used the Player Experience of Needs Satisfaction (PENS) scale, measuring Competence, Autonomy, and Relatedness.

To assess climate-related interest, we administered the Intrinsic Motivation Survey and the Situational Interest Survey after each game. These measures helped determine if playing Shloka fostered deeper interest and motivation toward climate action compared to the baseline games.

Participants

We recruited 24 participants (15 male, 9 female), aged between 20 and 55 years (M = 28.3, SD = 9.9), all practicing Hindus with varying degrees of religiosity. Participants were recruited from university communities, Hindu temples, and cultural gatherings. Incentives included holy food offerings and religious merchandise. All participants completed the surveys, and 14 participated in follow-up interviews.

Procedure

Each participant engaged in a structured three-hour session. Sessions began with an open conversation about climate change, followed by randomized gameplay using the Latin squares method to mitigate order effects. After each game, participants completed the engagement and motivation surveys.

To prevent cognitive carryover, participants watched a neutral three-minute video between gameplay sessions. Gameplay sessions were recorded, and semi-structured interviews were conducted afterward, focusing on reflections about climate change, game mechanics, and the role of rituals in gameplay.

Analysis

We used the Friedman test for repeated measures to analyze Likert-scale survey data, followed by post-hoc Wilcoxon signed-rank tests to compare Shloka against each baseline game. Qualitative data from interviews were analyzed using a grounded theory approach, with three researchers collaboratively developing and refining a coding scheme. An inter-coder reliability score of 0.76 was achieved.

Our results triangulate both statistical findings and qualitative insights, offering a comprehensive understanding of how religious themes and rituals shaped player experiences and interest in climate change.

One participant shared, “Chanting during the game reminded me of temple visits with my grandmother. It felt spiritual, not just like playing a game.”

Step 6: Data Analysis

Quantitative survey data were analyzed using Friedman tests for repeated measures, with Wilcoxon signed-rank tests for post-hoc comparisons. Interview transcripts were coded through grounded theory, revealing recurring themes of spiritual engagement, emotional reflection, and heightened climate awareness. This comprehensive analysis validated the effectiveness of integrating religious rituals in serious game design to promote pro-environmental thinking.

Key Insights

  1. Faith-based rituals foster deeper emotional reflection — Participants reported stronger personal meaning when interacting with culturally familiar mechanics like chanting or lighting a diya.
  2. Increased engagement and retention — Players spent more time completing levels in Shloka compared to a control group using a standard climate game.
  3. Spiritual alignment enhances climate awareness — Integrating moral teachings around stewardship helped reframe climate change as a spiritual responsibility.

Conclusion & Impact

Shloka demonstrates that religion can be a powerful design material in serious games—especially when addressing global issues like climate change. By connecting spiritual practices to environmental responsibility, the game fosters engagement that feels both culturally resonant and personally motivating. This work opens new directions for designing culturally grounded, faith-inspired learning experiences in HCI and game design.
