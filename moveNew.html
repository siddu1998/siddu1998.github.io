The Problem

Business Challenge: Invento Robotics faced critical inefficiencies in their robot development lifecycle. Communication breakdowns between Sales, Engineering, and Deployment teams created bottlenecks, delayed deployments, and frustrated clients.

Team Pain Points: Engineers had no visibility into what Sales promised clients. Deployment teams lacked technical documentation. Leadership couldn't track robot status without manually asking multiple teams. Information silos prevented effective collaboration.

Opportunity Cost: Each miscommunication delayed robot deployments by days, risked client dissatisfaction, and wasted engineering hours on preventable issues. Without systematic improvements, the startup couldn't scale operations.

My Approach: Researcher as Engineer

To gain authentic insights, I adopted an ethnographic approach, immersing myself as a "Researcher as Engineer." I worked directly within the software, hardware, and deployment teams, participating in daily activities while observing workflows, communication patterns, and pain points.

Why This Method?

As someone with an engineering background, I could credibly participate in technical work, gaining team trust and observing genuine behavior rather than idealized descriptions. This insider perspective revealed informal workarounds, unspoken frustrations, and the real gaps between documented processes and actual practice.

Key Findings: 5 Critical Bottlenecks

1. Information Silos

The Issue: Critical information about client requirements, robot configurations, and software updates failed to flow between teams. Sales made promises Engineering never heard about. Deployment teams arrived on-site without knowing robot specifications.

Impact: Repeated deployment failures, client frustration, wasted engineering time rebuilding features that were already complete but undocumented.

2. No Single Source of Truth

The Issue: Teams lacked centralized documentation for robot status, specifications, and history. Information lived in scattered emails, Slack messages, and individual memories.

What I Heard: "Yeah, I'll need to talk with the sales team to understand what they're selling... and the software team to see what features they have at this point..." â€” Manager describing typical information gathering

3. Inconsistent SOPs

The Issue: Standard Operating Procedures were either poorly documented, not followed, or unknown to relevant teams. Each deployment became a reinvention rather than following proven processes.

Impact: Knowledge loss when employees left, inconsistent quality, preventable errors during high-pressure deployments.

4. Technical Complexity Barriers

The Issue: Deployment teams needed to send ROS commands to robots but lacked programming expertise. They struggled with command-line interfaces during time-sensitive client installations.

What I Heard: "I can't send ROS commands, I don't get it... it has to be simple. Something like this..." â€” Deployment team member during co-design

5. Leadership Visibility Gaps

The Issue: Leadership had no aggregated view of robot performance, SOP compliance, or team bottlenecks. They relied on manual check-ins, preventing data-driven decision-making.

Impact: Reactive rather than proactive problem-solving, difficulty prioritizing resources, missed patterns across multiple robots.

Solution Concepts: Bridging the Gaps

Based on research findings, I developed low-fidelity prototypes addressing the core bottlenecks:

1. Centralized Robot Hub

  • Purpose: Single source of truth for all robot informationâ€”status, specs, client requirements, deployment history
  • Key Features: Real-time updates, team collaboration, version control for configurations
  • Impact: Eliminates information silos, reduces redundant communication, enables async collaboration

2. Simplified Deployment Interface

  • Purpose: Abstract complex ROS commands into user-friendly GUI for non-technical deployment teams
  • Key Features: Visual controls, pre-set configurations, error prevention, guided workflows
  • Impact: Reduces deployment errors, decreases training time, lowers technical barriers

3. Leadership Dashboard

  • Purpose: Aggregated view of robot fleet performance, team metrics, SOP compliance
  • Key Features: Status overview, performance analytics, bottleneck alerts
  • Impact: Enables data-driven decisions, proactive problem-solving, resource optimization

Research Insights & Learnings

What Worked Well

  • Ethnographic immersion: Being embedded as an engineer revealed authentic behaviors and pain points that interviews alone would have missed
  • Co-design approach: Quick sketching sessions empowered employees to articulate needs and visualize solutions
  • Multi-stakeholder perspective: Understanding all 5 teams prevented solving one group's problems while creating issues for another

Key Learnings

  • Startup chaos is systemic, not personal: Most communication failures stemmed from missing infrastructure, not individual negligence
  • Simple solutions win: Teams consistently chose simplicity over feature-richnessâ€”they wanted minimal clicks, maximum clarity
  • Trust enables honesty: Working alongside teams (not just interviewing) built trust that revealed real frustrations
  • Visual communication accelerates alignment: Sketching during co-design clarified needs faster than verbal descriptions

Impact & Recommendations

This research provided Invento Robotics with:

  • Documented bottlenecks: Clear evidence of where workflows break down and why
  • Prioritized solutions: Ranked improvements based on impact and feasibility
  • Visual prototypes: Tangible starting points for internal tool development
  • Process recommendations: Specific changes to improve cross-team communication without requiring new tools
